Characterization of Space Habitat Maintenance Ops with 10 Former Astronauts

Research Assistant @ University of Southern California • May 2022 to May 2023 • Domain: Human Factors

Summary: This project was a survey study to identify the types of feedback astronauts may receive from an AI mission control system, as well as the types of feedback astronauts may provide to AI mission control during maintenance operations. The study involved ten former astronauts from multiple nationalities, each with over 100 days in space and experience performing maintenance and operational tasks aboard the International Space Station between 2000 and 2015. The survey used fully structured interviews, in which astronauts responded to predetermined questions by selecting from provided options and, when desired, offering additional responses in their own words.

Skills: Survey design.

Stack: Qualtrics.

Project

NASA STRI HOME Capstone 3 Demonstration

Research Assistant @ University of Southern California • January 2024 to June 2024 • Domain: Project Management

Significance: This project was among the first collaborative research efforts to demonstrate how deep space maintenance operations can be conducted independently of Earth.

My Role
My administrative responsibilities included co-developing the space habitat scheduled maintenance scenario, co-developing demonstration requirements, coordinating between project owners (students, scientists, and professors), and integrating PhD and graduate-level projects to align with the scenario. My technical responsibility was to demonstrate a bi-directional assistance framework betweens humans and an AI-enable agents, with assistance quantified by the types and lengths of messages exchanged. Especially, this work highlighted the effectiveness of specific assistive actions (messages) from humans to the AI in the context of space habitat maintenance operations enhancing the AI's ability to provide better support.

Technical Implementation
TBD

A Problem Solution: A capstone demonstration is typically regarded as a presentation of a final product designed to address real-life disturbances. However, for fundamental research projects focused on future deep space habitats, demonstrating a final product is challenging, as it is often years from completion. To address this, I incorporated audience interaction from NASA reviewers, who introduced improvised issues into the maintenance task. Using the findings from my dissertation work, I demonstrated how the human-AI team collaborates to resolve proposed challenges by assisting each other.

Project

Pyro Igniter Integration for LOX-Kerosene Rocket Engine

Propulsion Engineer @ Liquid Propulsion Laboratory • August 2019 to September 2019 • Domain: Liquid Propulsion

Significance: This project was part of the qualification campaign for an additively manufactured rocket engine.

My Role
• Responsible for igniter integration and ignition system support during static-fire testing.

Technical Implementation
• Mechanical integration of the 3D-printed igniter housing.
• Electrical wiring of the ignition system (e-match) to the data acquisition (DAQ) box.

A Problem Solution: During static-fire testing, the engine initially failed to ignite due to a timing error. Because of the liquid oxygen (LOX) boil-off rate, we had only twenty minutes to re-integrate a fresh igniter and housing. Despite the time pressure, my two teammates and I remained calm and successfully completed the integration within the required timeframe. On the subsequent attempt, the engine ignited successfully, although the test ultimately ended in engine failure (see video below).
