Project

NASA STRI HOME Capstone 3 Demonstration

Summary: This capstone demonstration integrated multidisciplinary NASA-funded research efforts into a unified deep-space habitat ECLSS maintenance scenario. The scenario spanned planning through execution with AI and robotic systems. I served as the project integration lead and delivered the AI mission control demonstration.

Significance: This project was among the first large-scale collaborative research efforts involving twenty-six participants across six universities (UC Davis, CU Boulder, USC, CMU, Georgia Tech, and Texas A&M) to demonstrate how deep-space maintenance operations can be conducted independently of Earth.

My Role
• Co-led the full-day capstone event, including presentations and demonstrations for NASA reviewers.
• Co-developed the space habitat scheduled maintenance scenario.
• Co-developed demonstration requirements in coordination with the HOME director, former NASA astronaut Stephen K. Robinson, and NASA reviewers.
• Coordinated across project stakeholders (students, scientists, and faculty) to integrate projects into a unified operational scenario.
• Presented a human–AI interaction framework for space habitat maintenance operations.

Technical Implementation
• Set flowcharts to define the sequence of events and interactions in the demonstration.
• Co-designed and co-built a robotically manipulable non-functional ECLSS testbed.
• Set up and operated Open Broadcaster Software (OBS) to stream the demonstration to NASA reviewers and record the demonstration for post-analysis.
• Implemented a Python-based GUI to enable real-time assessment of human-AI interaction via computer vision during the demonstration.

A Problem Solution: Because future habitat systems are still under development, presenting a traditional final product was not feasible. I implemented an interactive capstone format with NASA reviewer-driven disturbances and applied my dissertation framework to assess human-AI interaction in real time under operational uncertainty.

Survey Study with 10 Former Astronauts

Summary: This study characterized maintenance support dynamics between astronauts and mission control in Earth-orbiting space habitats to inform future space operations with AI-enabled mission control agents. The findings directly informed the formulation of a human-AI interaction framework for space operations. I led the study design and data collection.

Significance: We surveyed ten former astronauts from multiple nationalities, each with over 100 days in space and experience performing maintenance and operational tasks aboard the International Space Station between 2000 and 2015. My Role

Technical Implementation

Project

Pyro Igniter Integration for LOX-Kerosene Rocket Engine

Summary: This project supported a static-fire test campaign of a LOX–kerosene engine through igniter integration. It achieved successful ignition prior to a structural failure of the engine. I collaborated with two graduate students on the igniter integration effort.

Significance: This project was part of the qualification campaign for an additively manufactured rocket engine.

My Role
• Responsible for igniter integration and ignition system support during static-fire testing.

Technical Implementation
• Mechanical integration of the 3D-printed igniter housing.
• Electrical wiring of the ignition system (e-match) to the data acquisition (DAQ) box.

A Problem Solution: During static-fire testing, the engine initially failed to ignite due to a timing error. Because of the liquid oxygen (LOX) boil-off rate, we had only twenty minutes to re-integrate a fresh igniter and housing. Despite the time pressure, my two teammates and I remained calm and successfully completed the integration within the required timeframe. On the subsequent attempt, the engine ignited successfully, although the test ultimately ended in engine failure (see video below).
